C I N E M A

The Start

The start of cinema took place in the late 18th century when inventors like Thomas Edison and the Lumière brothers developed inventions capable of capturing and projecting moving images. In 1895, the first public film screening took place where short, black-and-white films were showcased.

1920s

Silent film

In the early 1920s, silent films took center stage with famous pioneers like Charlie Chaplin and Buster Keaton. The films developed stories and characters that transcended language barriers and therefore conveyed narrative and emotion visually. The silent film era ended in the late 1920s, when synchronized sound was introduced, and sound films or “talkies” were born. The newest invention opened new possibilities for storytelling.

1930s & 1940s

Golden Age

This was the period when Hollywood was at the height of its golden age and dominated the film industry. It was during this time that many classic films were released, and many movie stars were at their peak, such as Marilyn Monroe, Audrey Hepburn, Cary Grant, and Humphrey Bogart. The development of film genres such as film noir and musicals were also a product of this period. Films during the Great Depression and World War II provided escapism for audiences, it would give people something to feel good about and help people forget their troubles. The Golden Age also corresponds with a period after the Great Depression in which the US was booming economically. People flocked to Hollywood to become actors or actresses and the influx resulted in a larger number of high-quality films being made, making this period successful in terms of filmmaking.

1950s & 1960s

This period introduced the rise of international cinema, with influential filmmakers like Akira Kurosawa and Frederico Fellini. It was a period full of new storytelling techniques and extraordinary performances. In the 1950s, the advent of color film and the proliferation of television brought about changes in the industry, prompting filmmakers to experiment with new visual styles and storytelling techniques.

1970s

New Hollywood

The 1970s became a creative high point in the film industry. It was the start of the mindset of film as art and marked a period of artistic and cultural revolution in cinema. The restrictions had loosened up, and the hippie movement, the civil rights movement, free love, rock ‘n’ roll, changing gender roles, and drug use certainly had an impact. New Hollywood, a movement where a new generation of young filmmakers came to prominence, came to rise during this time. Directors like Martin Scorsese, Francis Ford Coppola, and Steven Spielberg were significant during this period with films like “Taxi Driver” (1976), “The Godfather” trilogy, and “Jaws” (1975). These films pushed the boundaries of storytelling, tackling complex themes and pushing the limits of technical innovation.

1980s

The 1980s were characterized by the introduction of “high-concept” films – they had short cinematic plots that could be summarized in one or two sentences. The films in the 80s were less experimental and original than the 70s, but more formulaic. New special effects (CGI) were introduced which films were eager to capitalize on. Directors like Steven Spielberg and George Lucas are associated with the 80s and some famous films are “E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial” (1982), “Back to the Future” (1985), “Ghostbusters” (1984), and “Indiana Jones”.

The 21st century

The film industry has become more spectacular and mesmerizing than ever. Digital technology of the 21st century has resulted in groundbreaking advancements in visual effects, animation, and virtual reality. Technologies like 3D and CGI are blending the real world with the reel one. Streaming services, like Netflix and Disney+, have changed the way films are produced and distributed and have expanded opportunities for diverse and niche content and filmmakers. Famous directors of the 21st century are Quentin Tarantino, Christopher Nolan, David Fincher, and Denis Villeneuve. Some famous films are “Interstellar”, “Avatar”, “Joker”, “Shutter Island”, “Prisoners”, “Zodiac”, and “The Dark Knight”.
